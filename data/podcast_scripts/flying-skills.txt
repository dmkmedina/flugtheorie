# Flying Skills
# One turn per line as 'Name: text'. Lines starting with # are ignored.
# Hosts: Maya (instructor) and Theo (curious learner).

Theo: Okay Maya, the aerodynamics episode was all forces and arrows. This one's the doing part, right? Actually flying the thing.
Maya: It is. And here's the frame to carry the whole way through: a flight doesn't start at the launch site. It starts at your kitchen table the night before.
Theo: The kitchen table? That feels like a stretch.
Maya: Half of flying safely is decisions you make before you ever clip in. We'll take the journey in order: prepare, launch, manage the air, deal with trouble, land. And the first question at that table is about you, not the weather. Are you fit to fly? Clear-headed, slept well, no painkiller masking some injury. Your body is part of the aircraft, and up high it gets tested.
Theo: Tested how? What actually goes wrong with the body up there?
Maya: The big one is lack of oxygen, hypoxia. The air is always one fifth oxygen, but as you climb the pressure drops, so every breath delivers less. Two kinds matter. Instantaneous hypoxia starts from around four thousand metres, and the cruel part is the first symptom: euphoria. You feel wonderful just as your judgement falls apart, then fatigue, confusion, and by seven thousand metres, convulsions and worse. The other is acute mountain sickness, building slowly over hours from about two thousand eight hundred metres. But the treatment for any of it is one thing: lose altitude, fast.
Theo: Good to know it's one simple fix. What else does the body throw at us?
Maya: Cold, sneakier than you'd think, because moving air steals heat far faster than still air. The number the exam loves: flying at thirty-two kilometres an hour in air that's two degrees feels like minus eleven on your skin. That costs you energy and sharpness, so dress warm and drink, because airflow dries you out too.
Theo: Suppose the worst has happened and I come across a crashed pilot. What's the drill?
Maya: Three letters: A, B, C. Airway, Breathing, Circulation. Is the airway clear, are they breathing, is there bleeding or a pulse. Unconscious but breathing, roll them into the recovery position so they can't choke. But one case is the opposite of helping: a suspected spine injury, with back pain or limb numbness. Because we land seated, straight onto the spine, it's sadly common, and you do not move them, don't sit them up, don't even give a drink, because wrong handling can mean permanent paralysis. Keep them still, call mountain rescue. And when the helicopter comes, stop all flying and weight down loose fabric with stones, or the downwash flings it into the rotor.
Theo: Right, back to the kitchen table. Weather. There's a memory hook, isn't there?
Maya: The F-F-G rule. Three hazards: Föhn, that dry violent alpine wind. Front, a weather front. And Gewitter, the German for thunderstorm. If any one shows up in the forecast, your ears grow very big, because all three bring the single biggest killer in this sport, strong wind and turbulence. We fly slowly, so wind hurts us more than almost anything.
Theo: So weather's hazard one of the home checklist. What are the others?
Maya: Four in total. Weather, then the legal and airspace picture, then obstacles and terrain, then wildlife. For airspace you check the DABS, the daily bulletin, every flying day, because restrictions change, and a big event like the World Economic Forum can close huge volumes of sky. And wildlife is heavily on the exam: spring is when we fly highest, exactly when animals raise their young. Eagles are on the nest March to May, chamois have their young in May, ibex in June. See animals beneath you, fly away. Don't tell yourself the thermal's too good.
Theo: Fair. I drive to the hill. Where do I look first?
Maya: Counterintuitively, the landing field, ideally before you even go up. That's where the ground is closest and the dangers most immediate. Walk it, find the cables, the big trees, learn the pattern, usually left-hand but check the sign or ask a local. And always have a plan B landing in mind in case the main field gets closed.
Theo: Now the launch. There's one golden rule, isn't there?
Maya: One above all others: from any launch site, at any moment, you must be able to abort the take-off safely. A cliff with three steps of run and then a drop breaks that rule, and it's the wrong site.
Theo: How do I picture "able to abort"? It feels vague.
Maya: Two imaginary lines on the slope. The decision line: if the wing isn't perfect by the time you reach it, you stop, and it's usually less than ten metres in front of where you start inflating. Further down, the point of no return, which the instructors bluntly call the life line. Past it, you simply must already be flying.
Theo: Decision line, then life line. And before all that, the famous check.
Maya: The five-point check, in order, every flight, and you remember it because it radiates outward from your body. One, equipment: leg straps and chest strap closed, helmet on, reserve handle in place. Two, risers and lines, untwisted, brakes in hand. Three, the wing, open and facing the wind. Four, the wind itself, direction and strength. Five, furthest out, the airspace and your launch path, clear of pilots and clear of children or cattle.
Theo: Equipment, risers, wing, wind, airspace. And there's a tip about laying the wing out?
Maya: Lay it in a shallow arc, not a straight line. That way the central lines come tight a hair before the tips, and the wing inflates evenly, instead of one corner snapping up first.
Theo: Now the run. People talk about a three-phase launch.
Maya: Three phases, and the discipline is to treat them as separate. Phase one, inflation: you pull the A risers and bring the wing up overhead. Phase two, check and correct: is it square, level, fully open, no knots? Phase three, accelerate: you commit forward, build speed, and let it lift you off. The mistake is mentally skipping ahead, already flying in your head before the wing's even up.
Theo: So when exactly is it too late to abort?
Maya: The decision to go is always made before phase three. Aborting in phase one or two is clean, no problem, even in the exam. Aborting in phase three means you didn't check properly, and that's where it gets dangerous, because once you're accelerating you're committed.
Theo: Walk me through what the hands and weight actually do through those phases.
Maya: In phase one you lean forward to keep the lines tight and let the wing rise behind you, easing the energy a touch so you can feel it come up. In phase two the hands come onto the brakes, roughly half way, to stop it overshooting, and you glance up to check it. Phase three, lean forward again, accelerate hard, legs down ready to cushion you if you touch back. You only sit into the harness once you've got plenty of air beneath you.
Theo: And if the wing comes up crooked, leaning to one side?
Maya: Fix it in phase two with two combined actions. Say it rises tilted left. You apply the right brake to level the wing, and you also step across to the left, back underneath it, as you run. Brake the high side, move under the wing.
Theo: What about wind that isn't a nice headwind on the nose?
Maya: The ideal is a steady headwind around fifteen kilometres an hour, which slows your groundspeed and shortens your run. A tailwind is the dangerous one: more than five and you abort, because you can't get enough air over the wing. A crosswind, you inflate facing into the wind, then pivot toward the slope before accelerating. And really strong wind, twenty to twenty-five, you switch to a reverse launch, facing the wing. The point people forget there: check what's behind you, because strong wind can drag you backward, so a cliff behind your launch is a disaster waiting to happen.
Theo: Reverse launch confuses me. Walk me through the body.
Maya: It's choreography. You clip in with the wing behind you, rotate to face it, leaning back, lifting one set of risers over your head so the lines cross cleanly at riser height, brake handles in the matching hands. Five-point check facing the wing, then pull the A risers, leaning back against the wind, and the big advantage is you watch the whole wing come up. When it's overhead, drop the risers onto the brakes, turn back to the slope, accelerate. And the instructor stressed: after the check, repeat point five just before you commit, because you've turned around and the airspace might have changed.
Theo: Say the wing's up but I'm drifting toward the hill. What's the priority?
Maya: This is the most important reflex in the whole launch, what the instructor calls the unofficial fourth point. Before you diagnose anything, stabilise your direction: weight firmly to the correct side, opposite brake enough to fly straight, and fly out, away from terrain, building distance. First fly the wing, then solve the problem. Never turn back toward the slope to investigate.
Theo: Why does distance matter so much?
Maya: Distance is time. The bigger the gap, the calmer you work the problem. Say you've got a knot you missed. A knot shortens that side and deforms the profile, so the wing pulls toward the knotted side and you hold straight with opposite brake.
Theo: Can I clear a knot in the air?
Maya: Sometimes, in order. First, an impulse on the brake on the knot side. If that fails, pull the stabilo line, the thin outermost trim line. If that fails, induce a controlled fifty percent collapse on that side to shake it loose. And if none of it works and you can't guarantee a safe landing, then while you can still steer, fly out over a big open meadow, not woods, and throw the reserve. Better that than wrestling an unsteerable wing toward the ground.
Theo: Let's say it's a clean flight. Now I'm up. How do I fly to go far versus stay up long? I always mix those.
Maya: Different speeds, just like in aerodynamics. To go farthest in calm air, fly best glide, hands almost all the way up, maybe five to fifteen centimetres of brake. To stay airborne longest, fly minimum sink, twenty to thirty percent brake. Slowest down, not furthest forward. And wind shifts all of it over the ground. Four rules: into headwind or sinking air, speed up, push accelerator, or you'll never make it. Picture flying thirty into a thirty headwind, you're standing still until you accelerate and claw back ten. With a tailwind, ease off and let it carry you. In lift, fly minimum sink to milk the climb.
Theo: There's also this crab thing for crosswind.
Maya: Yes, and the trap is what the instructor calls the dog-leg. If a crosswind pushes you sideways and you just point your nose at the target, you get blown off, swing wide, arrive low. Instead you set a wind-correction angle: aim the wing's nose slightly upwind so your track over the ground runs straight to the goal. Stronger crosswind, bigger angle; faster you fly, smaller angle. Set it once, don't keep chasing the target.
Theo: Now staying up. You mentioned ridge soaring and thermals.
Maya: Two ways. Ridge soaring first: wind hits a slope, gets forced up, and that band of lift holds you as you fly back and forth along the ridge. The rule that keeps you alive: always turn away from the ridge, into the wind, never toward it, or you lose height with a tailwind and sink from the turn and end up in the rock or rotor. The mantra for all soaring is three words: terrain clearance, terrain clearance, terrain clearance.
Theo: Because you're flying right next to the hill the whole time.
Maya: With other pilots, in air that can suddenly roughen. Now thermals, the real art: a column of warm air rising off the ground that you bank into and circle to climb. To centre it, feel the climb rate around your circle: increasing, ease the turn; decreasing, tighten. Fall out entirely, and a sharp one-eighty back usually finds it again.
Theo: Any reflex people get wrong in a thermal?
Maya: Most pilots over-use the brakes and under-use their weight. Lead with the body, shift onto the turn side first, only then add brake. Before any turn, check the airspace you're turning toward. Wind tilts the column downwind, so the best lift sits upwind of the cloud above it, sink lurks downwind. And don't leave a climb until you've picked your next target and have the height to reach it. Mountain thermals especially are crisp, narrow and rough off a steep face, so match your bank angle to the width of the core.
Theo: Okay, the part I'm nervous about. When the wing misbehaves.
Maya: Let's build it up calmly, because calm is the whole skill. First, posture in rough air: stay supple, keep a little brake on to feel the wing and firm it up, and don't over-react. The wing surges and rolls, you damp it gently.
Theo: Start with the most common scare. A collapse.
Maya: An asymmetric collapse, where one side tucks. Say half the wing folds. It looks violent but it's not dangerous if you respond right. The danger is doing nothing, because a collapsed side creates drag and turns toward the collapse, and left alone it winds up into a spiral.
Theo: So what do my hands and body do the instant it folds?
Maya: First stabilise, then react. Weight immediately to the open, flying side, a little brake with the opposite hand, just enough to hold your heading straight. Don't fix the collapse yet, fix your direction. Once you're tracking straight and away from the hill, let the folded side re-open, pumping gently if you must. And a subtlety on leaning: in theory and on the exam, you weight the open side, that's the answer to give. In practice with a big collapse the instructor's tip is to just let your body weight fall naturally, because gravity loads the side that's still flying, which helps it re-inflate.
Theo: Now the deliberate stuff. People intentionally fold the wing to come down. Big ears.
Maya: You reach up and pull the outermost A lines down on each side, and the tips fold in. The wing keeps flying forward and level, no extra G, but your sink jumps, three to nearly four metres a second for little ears, even more for big. There's one rule that's non-negotiable: you must do it on the accelerator, pushing the bar at the same time.
Theo: Why is the bar so important?
Maya: A lovely bit of physics. Folding the tips shrinks your wing area, which raises your wing loading and pushes your stall point up, maybe from eighteen kilometres an hour to twenty-five. And the folded ears raise your angle of attack and slow you down, so your margin to the stall collapses from both ends. Pushing the bar tilts the nose down, speeds you up, restores the gap. Ears and bar go together, always. To get out, come off the bar a little, no more than half, release the A lines, and if the ears stay tucked, pump them, still on the bar, only coming fully off once they're open.
Theo: Why pick ears over other ways down?
Maya: Because you keep flying forward, so you can escape a band of lift while you sink. Compare the B-stall, where you pull both B risers down together, breaking the airflow over the top. The wing stops flying forward and sinks almost straight down, about seven metres a second, barely any effort, but you come down on the spot, can't fly out of anything. There's a grip detail, the comb grip: spread your fingers between the B lines, above the line-locks, so the risers can't slip out. Pull slowly but firmly, hold at least three or four seconds.
Theo: And the exit?
Maya: This is where people get hurt. You must not let the risers snap up. If they fly up and you accidentally catch the brakes while the wing's still stalled, you command it straight into backward flight. So you actively guide the risers up, let the wing return to forward flight, and only then hands fully up.
Theo: This keeps coming back to the stall. Tell me about the stall point itself.
Maya: One of the most important things in the sport, because mishandling it is the second most common cause of accidents. The stall point is where you've pulled the brakes so deep the wing stops flying forward. It announces itself two ways, and you react to whichever you notice first.
Theo: Two ways?
Maya: One is pressure. As you pull deeper the brake pressure rises and rises, then suddenly drops, the instructor calls it like a pistol shot, bang, the pressure's gone. The other is visual: the wing's underside, taut as you pull, suddenly bulges upward as air pushes from below. They don't always happen at the same instant.
Theo: So the moment I feel either one?
Maya: Hands up, briskly. The golden rule the instructor repeats: when in doubt, hands up. You lose nothing by easing off too early, and everything by stalling near the ground. And if you get stuck back there in a deep stall, move your hands forward onto the A risers, pull, and release, which forces the wing back into forward flight.
Theo: Let me get two words straight that I always confuse. Spin and twist.
Maya: Different things entirely. A spin is when you over-brake one side, usually the inside of a turn, and that half of the wing flies backward while the other flies forward. The warning sign is the brake suddenly going slack on the inside. You recover by bringing both hands fully up. A twist is when you, the pilot, get rotated and your risers cross above you. The wing's fine, you just can't steer until you untwist.
Theo: There are training manoeuvres named after the axes too. Pitch and roll.
Maya: They teach you to manage the wing's swing. Pitching is the fore-and-aft surge: into a thermal the wing pitches back, and leaving it, it dives forward, and you bring it back over your head. The secret is timing, not strength. After it surges forward you wait, let it swing through the bottom and start climbing on the far side before braking again, because brake on the down-swing and you kill the energy you built. Rolling is the same idea side to side, but the order is the headline: weight first, brake later. Your body crosses to the new side before the brake follows.
Theo: Okay, the absolute last resort. The reserve.
Maya: First thing to burn in: it is completely useless unless you pull it. We have no automatic system like skydivers. The instructor calls it the second chance. Pull it when the situation is uncontrollable and won't settle within two or three seconds. Or when you're low and have a problem, immediately. Or after any collision, because you may not know your wing's damaged until you're on final and it's too late.
Theo: And after I throw it?
Maya: Pull in the B or C lines on your main wing so it stops flying and tangling with the reserve. Then sit up, knees bent and together, ready to land with a roll, because you'll come down around five to six metres a second. And a habit for every flight: touch the reserve handle with a flat hand so you know exactly where it is without looking.
Theo: Bring me home, literally. The landing.
Maya: The landing pattern, the volte, a U-shape, and we all fly the same shape so we can predict each other near the ground where there's no time to react. Four phases. One, descending three-sixties beside or upwind of the field, bleeding height to about fifty to seventy metres. Two, the downwind leg, parallel to the field. Three, the crosswind base, across. Four, final, straight into the wind to your spot.
Theo: Any detail people get wrong?
Maya: Your descending circles in phase one must turn the same direction as your pattern. Left-hand pattern, left-hand circles. And throughout the whole approach, eyes fixed on your landing target, only quick glances at obstacles. Staring at an obstacle is dangerous, because the wing drifts to wherever you're looking.
Theo: What if I misjudge my height? Too high on the downwind?
Maya: Enlarge the base leg, more room to bleed it off. Too low, shorten it. On final, too high, make yourself big, sit up out of the harness, legs ready, to add drag, but careful, more than eighty percent brake risks a stall right by the ground. Too low on final, hands up for max glide, brake only just before you touch. And if the wind isn't on the nose like you expected, commit to the pattern you chose, don't change it late. With a tailwind you come in faster and flatter, so you brake more decisively, a bit higher, building the flare so the wing stands you up just as you touch.
Theo: Strong wind landings scare me. What's different there?
Maya: Twenty-five and up, the priority is the moment after you touch. You immediately turn one-eighty to face the wing, run downwind toward it, and pull the B or C risers to kill its lift so it can't drag you. And whatever you do, do not brake, because braking increases lift and the wing can pluck you back off the ground.
Theo: What about landing somewhere awkward, like a slope?
Maya: On a slope you never land straight up the hill, that's far too brutal a contact. You land parallel to the slope contour, or across it, and you can flare normally. And steer clear of obstacles upwind of your field, because a building or a line of trees throws mechanical turbulence a long way downwind.
Theo: Worst case. I can't reach a field at all. What do I aim for?
Maya: A priority order, best to worst: a tree, then a sloped roof, then water. Water is last, because of drowning, lines tangling, cold. If water's unavoidable, unclip your harness before you touch it, but never jump out early, because in the adrenaline you can't judge whether you're five metres up or twenty.
Theo: And a tree, since it's the best of the bad options?
Maya: Pick a conifer, protect your head with your arms, bleed off speed with the brakes on the way in. Then, the key bit, once you're hung up there, stay put, roped or waiting for rescue. The number one injury in tree landings is people unclipping in shock, thinking it's a short drop, and falling much further than they judged.
Theo: That was a lot of ground. Can you tie the whole thing together?
Maya: Here's the spine. Flying starts at the kitchen table: are you fit, what's the weather, the airspace, the terrain, the wildlife. On the hill, you must always be able to abort, so respect the decision line; check five points in order, launch in three phases, never commit before the wing is right. In the air, first fly the wing, then solve the problem, every time. Speed up into headwind and sink, slow down with a tailwind, minimum sink in lift. When trouble comes, stabilise your direction before you fix the wing, and when in doubt, hands up. And land by flying a disciplined pattern with your eyes locked on your spot.
Theo: First fly the wing, then fix the problem. When in doubt, hands up. Always be able to abort. I think those three alone would keep me out of most trouble. That actually all connects now. Thanks Maya.
Maya: They genuinely would. Everything else is detail hanging off those bones, exactly like the aerodynamics. Knowledge first, then training, then skill, never skipping a step. Fly the wing, make the decision early, stay calm. Catch you in the next episode.
