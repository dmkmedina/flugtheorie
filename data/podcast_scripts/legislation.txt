# Law & Airspace

# Hosts: Maya (instructor) and Theo (curious learner).

Theo: Confession, Maya. The legislation chapter sits at the back of the booklet, and that's exactly where my brain files it. Boring, skippable, cram it the night before. Tell me why I'm wrong.
Maya: Because these rules aren't bureaucracy. They're a collision-avoidance system written in plain language. Every altitude, every distance, every who-yields-to-whom exists because somewhere, sometime, two aircraft nearly met. Learn it as safety, not paperwork, and it gets interesting.
Theo: Fair. So who actually makes the rules I'm flying under?
Maya: In Switzerland, only the Confederation, the federal state itself, can legislate on the use of the airspace. But it delegates the day-to-day to one federal office. You'll meet it in three languages on the exam, so let's say all three: in English, the Federal Office of Civil Aviation; in French, OFAC; in German, BAZL. The Confederation writes the law, that office enforces it.
Theo: And the specific law that covers paragliders?
Maya: The OACS, the Ordinance on Aircraft of Special Categories, in force since nineteen ninety-four. It governs the whole family of foot-launched wings: deltas, rigid wings, paragliders. Legally we're slope gliders, and the definition is simple: a machine you launch on foot at running speed, used right after launch for gliding and soaring near a slope.
Theo: Here's what surprises me. My car has a number plate and a roadworthiness test. My wing has neither?
Maya: Almost nothing, and that's genuinely unusual in aviation. Your wing is in no aircraft register, and there's no compulsory airworthiness inspection. A check every couple of years is recommended, never required, and the pilot carries primary responsibility for the wing's airworthiness. Even a helmet and reserve parachute are mandatory only for your practical exam flights, never for everyday flying.
Theo: So what is actually compulsory?
Maya: Three things, and each hides an exam trap. One, liability insurance. Two, an identification number on the wing. Three, a manufacturer's plate. Insurance first: at least one million Swiss francs covering damage to uninvolved third parties on the ground. Picture it, you land, your lines drag a branch down onto a parked car. Your private liability won't pay that. This will.
Theo: One million, third parties, on the ground. And if the damage costs more?
Maya: You personally pay the difference, it's a floor, not a ceiling on your responsibility. And here's the trap examiners love: damage to another aircraft in flight is not covered by that minimum. Only ground damage. That's why the Federation offers an enhanced policy up to two million that does include in-flight damage. If you ever leave the Federation, read what the cover includes, don't just chase the cheapest premium.
Theo: Noted. Now the wing number.
Maya: Five or more digits, easily recognisable, on the underside of the wing, and here's the figure that sticks: each digit forty centimetres tall. The Federation allocates them, and it's usually the same as your licence number. Every wing you fly carries that same personal number, and it must match the number on your insurance certificate. Examiners test that match.
Theo: And the manufacturer's plate? What's on it?
Maya: A small patch sewn into an edge or wingtip. Four items: the maker's name, the type of wing, the year of manufacture, and the minimum and maximum loads. That's the lot. The serial number and the certification class are not mandatory, that's the trap there.
Theo: Good. So what must I physically carry every flight?
Maya: Two documents: your insurance certificate, number matching the wing, and your pilot licence. And know who can demand them, officials of the Federal Office of Civil Aviation, the police, and aerodrome managers.
Theo: Quick licensing question while we're here. How old, and does the licence expire?
Maya: Minimum sixteen to hold it, school from fifteen. And it has unlimited duration. No medical exam, ever, no upper age limit, no minimum flights per year. Once you have your brevet, it doesn't lapse, though a refresher after a long break is just common sense.
Theo: One more before we climb. Tandems.
Maya: Two licences. Tandem A lets you take any passenger, even someone with no licence. Tandem B is narrower: your passenger must already hold a solo licence in the same category, so a tandem paraglider B pilot can't take a licensed delta pilot. And never more than one passenger.
Theo: Right. Now take me up. The airspace genuinely scares me on the chart.
Maya: It scared every instructor too. The trick they all teach: build it from the ground up, one layer at a time. Worldwide, the body called ICAO, the International Civil Aviation Organization, defined seven airspace classes, A through G. Switzerland uses four: C, D, E and G. A phrase to remember them, Ceiling Defines Every Glide.
Theo: And the big difference between them?
Maya: E and G are uncontrolled, the only spaces where a paraglider flies freely, no clearance, no radio. C and D are controlled: C needs clearance and continuous radio, D needs clearance though radio isn't strictly required. We stay out of both. The only realistic case is clipping an approach corridor on a long cross-country, where you might radio in and sometimes be granted a transit.
Theo: So I basically live in E and G. Start at the bottom.
Maya: Picture a carpet laid over the whole of Switzerland, draped over every valley and peak. That's airspace G, six hundred metres thick. And because it's measured above the ground, Above Ground Level, AGL, its ceiling rises and falls with the land underneath. Hold that thought, it pays off shortly.
Theo: Above the carpet?
Maya: You step into airspace E, still uncontrolled, still ours. How high E reaches before it meets controlled airspace depends on where you stand, because of one line across the country. It runs from Lake Geneva, around Montreux, across central Switzerland to the Alpstein and on into Austria. North and west: the Jura and the Mittelland, the low plateau. South: the Alps, where everything sits higher, including the ceilings.
Theo: So give me those ceilings.
Maya: In the Jura and Mittelland, the top of E is three thousand and fifty metres above sea level, and it doesn't care whether the military flies. Fixed. In the Alps it depends on the military: when the military is off, four thousand five hundred and fifty metres; when it's active, it drops to three thousand nine hundred and fifty. The chart shows MIL ON at the top when the air force is up.
Theo: When's the military on?
Maya: Precise hours, Monday to Friday, oh seven thirty-five to twelve oh five, and thirteen fifteen to seventeen oh five. The odd five-minute buffers are deliberate, to stop overlap with civil traffic. And holidays count as military off, so the alpine ceiling stays high.
Theo: Jura three thousand and fifty always, Alps four five five oh off, three nine five oh on. Now, that carpet trick.
Maya: Here it is, a favourite exam question. Because G is six hundred metres thick above the ground, the carpet rides up over every summit. Take a peak four thousand two hundred metres high. Six hundred metres of carpet on top means you may legally climb to four thousand eight hundred metres above it.
Theo: But that's above the four five five oh alpine ceiling.
Maya: It is, and it's still legal, as long as your ground clearance never exceeds six hundred metres, you're in the G carpet the whole way up. The instant the terrain drops away and you're more than six hundred metres above ground, the four five five oh limit snaps back.
Theo: Sneaky but logical. Now those numbers, three thousand and fifty, three nine five oh. You called them flight levels.
Maya: Altitude in hundreds of feet measured against a fixed standard pressure, one thousand and thirteen point two five hectopascals. It exists because real pressure changes daily, so altimeters drift, and above a certain height everyone agrees to fly by the same standard instead. What matters is that aircraft are separated, not their exact metres. Flight level one hundred is ten thousand feet, and since a foot is thirty point four eight centimetres, that's three thousand and forty-eight metres, rounded to three thousand and fifty. Your Jura ceiling. Quick trick, multiply the flight level by thirty for rough metres.
Theo: So the alpine ceilings are just flight levels too. And on the chart, how do I tell a true height from a standard-pressure one?
Maya: Three clues that carry enormous weight. Upright numbers are above the ground, AGL. Italic numbers are above sea level, AMSL. And a number highlighted in blue is a flight level, standard pressure, the one that can drift, up to roughly two hundred metres either way in extreme pressure. Good news, in a strong high the boundaries move up, giving us more room. A question might throw a monstrous high at you and bait you to calculate the Jura ceiling, but don't, it shifts at most about two hundred metres.
Theo: Upright is ground, italic is sea level, blue is standard pressure. Now airspace D, where's that?
Maya: Mainly around airfields. The control zone, the CTR, sits as airspace D from the ground up around a runway. Above it, terminal manoeuvring areas, TMAs, stack like boxes. A structural detail examiners probe: a TMA's lower limit is a fixed altitude, because of the terrain underneath, while its upper limit floats as a flight level.
Theo: Why fixed at the bottom, floating at the top?
Maya: Picture a TMA over a valley. If its floor drifted with the pressure, on a low day it could sink into the mountains and an airliner would meet rock. So the floor is nailed to a true altitude; up high there are no obstacles, so it can float. Above the TMAs, airways, the AWYs, connect the airports, upper limit flight level one ninety-five. And these zones have three activity states: the digit nine means permanently active, like Zurich; the letters HX mean no fixed hours, query before you go near; and temporary zones are announced daily, only Sion and Alpnach in all of Switzerland.
Theo: You hinted there's a relief for us around TMAs.
Maya: A precious one, and a classic trap. South of the dividing line only, in the Alps, you may fly underneath a TMA up to three hundred metres above the ground without clearance. That keeps valleys like Meiringen flyable, where the TMA floor would otherwise seal the airspace shut. North, in the Mittelland, the relief doesn't exist. So a question might box you under a TMA in Meiringen, tempting you to tick forbidden, but you're in the Alps at a hundred and fifty metres above ground, perfectly legal, just follow the terrain and stay within three hundred metres of it.
Theo: Now airfields and heliports. How far must I stay?
Maya: Without other information, five kilometres from an airfield runway, civil or military, with no control zone, and two and a half kilometres from a heliport. But that protective cylinder is only six hundred metres tall above the ground, so higher than that you may overfly an airfield, no violation. And some sites have local agreements, a wedge cut out, like Trogen, not on the chart. If your route grazes a heliport, check the Federation's local-agreements list, it can make an illegal line legal.
Theo: There were coloured zones too, red and green.
Maya: Two different LS-R meanings that confusingly share the letters. Plain LS-R, red on the chart, is restricted, forbidden to enter when active. LS-D is a danger area, entering isn't strictly forbidden but it's discouraged, because something hazardous like live firing may be happening below. The green outline is the gift: LS-R for Gliders, restricted only to instrument-flight airline traffic but open to us, with much smaller cloud clearances inside. But mind the calendar, the green zones are active only from the first of March to the thirty-first of October.
Theo: And outside those dates?
Maya: No effect, which is exactly what a winter examiner exploits, a beautiful February thermal day where you assume the green zone helps and it isn't even switched on. Lock the dates.
Theo: This all assumes I can find out a zone's status. Where do I look?
Maya: Four publications at four timescales. First, the Glider Chart, the GLDC, a topographical map made for us, showing the airspace classes, hazard zones, obstacles, the green soaring zones, the dividing line and the military hours. New edition every March. Keep a paper copy for overview, but always re-check the digital version before flying, on the federal viewer, map dot geo dot admin dot ch, with its legend. Second, the AIP, the Aeronautical Information Publication, the master long-form reference describing every airspace and aerodrome in full, updated monthly.
Theo: So the chart is the picture, the AIP the full text. What about right now, today?
Maya: That's the third, and the single most important pre-flight check: the DABS, the Daily Airspace Bulletin Switzerland, published every day at skybriefing dot com. It lists what's actually active today, the real military status, live restricted zones, the temporary TMAs, parachute drops, firing ranges. Two rookie mistakes: first, check the displayed date is the day you intend to fly; second, the times are in UTC, so for Swiss local time add one hour in winter, two in summer. Forget that and a zone you thought opened at oh seven hundred actually opens at oh nine hundred.
Theo: Date first, then UTC plus one or two. And the fourth source?
Maya: The NOTAM, the Notice to Airmen, a daily source of short-term changes, airspace activations, hazards, ground obstacles. For us the relevant slice is the VFR-NOTAM. And military shooting times have their own daily bulletin, the KOSIF in German, COTSENA in French.
Theo: Good. Now the part that's really about not dying. First, what rules am I flying under?
Maya: Visual Flight Rules, VFR, always. You fly by sight. The alternative, Instrument Flight Rules, IFR, needs an instrument-equipped aircraft and a specially rated pilot. No matter how many instruments you strap to your harness, a paraglider can never fly IFR, and never into cloud. For us, VFR, full stop.
Theo: And VFR sets how far I see and stay from cloud?
Maya: Yes, and it changes with height above ground. Below three hundred metres above the ground, that's only the lower half of the G carpet, not the full six hundred, the rule is gentle: one and a half kilometres horizontal visibility, outside cloud at all times, ground always in sight, and you must always be able to make a one-hundred-and-eighty-degree turn. No numerical cloud distance at all.
Theo: And above three hundred metres?
Maya: The strict rule bites, throughout the upper half of G and all of airspace E: three hundred metres vertically and one thousand five hundred metres horizontally from any cloud. Visibility five kilometres below three thousand and fifty metres, eight kilometres above. The higher visibility is because faster traffic can burst out of cloud up there, and you need more time to see and avoid.
Theo: And the green glider zones change that?
Maya: Inside an active LS-R for Gliders the cloud distances shrink to fifty metres vertical and one hundred horizontal, but the visibility minima, five and eight, and the see-the-ground rule stay. And here's a favourite trap. Say the zone's upper limit is two thousand four hundred and fifty metres and a thermal carries you to two thousand seven hundred. You may keep climbing, the altitude isn't a wall, but you're now above the zone, so the large cloud distances return. People trip thinking the upper limit caps the flying, it only caps the reduced clearances.
Theo: Is there a checklist to tie this together at launch?
Maya: The instructors give five questions on takeoff. One, season, am I between the first of March and the thirty-first of October so the green zones are live? Two, day and time, is the military on, and how high may I climb? Three, which side of the dividing line am I, Mittelland or Alps? Four, my altitude right now, both above ground and above sea level? Five, am I inside the radius of any restricted, danger or aerodrome zone? Answer those, and you can answer almost any exam question.
Theo: Season, military, side of the line, altitude, location. Now right-of-way, the heart of it.
Maya: The whole system ranks aircraft by manoeuvrability, and the rule is humane: the less manoeuvrable aircraft has priority, because it can't easily get out of the way. Picture a ladder, top down. An aircraft in distress at the very top. Then a balloon. Then us, paragliders, hang gliders and sailplanes, all equal. Then an airship, then a towing motor aircraft, and at the bottom, the most manoeuvrable, powered aircraft and helicopters.
Theo: So a helicopter yields to me?
Maya: When you're where you belong, yes. But a sharp caveat: asserting right-of-way over an airliner inside a TMA is no defence in court. Priority only protects you in airspace you're entitled to use.
Theo: Two of us, same class, paths crossing?
Maya: Same as the road, the one on the right has priority. And head-on, both of you turn right. Overtaking is its own rule: it's defined by an angle, approaching from within the rear one-hundred-and-forty-degree arc, seventy degrees from the tail on each side, counts as overtaking, and the overtaking aircraft never has priority, because the one ahead can't easily see behind. Overtaking is allowed in free airspace but forbidden on a slope.
Theo: Now the thermal. Two of us circling, which way?
Maya: The first glider into the core sets the direction, not the lowest, not the highest, the first one in. Everyone joining turns the same way. Why it matters: a faster wing can climb up from below and catch you, and circling opposite ways you'd meet head-on at a huge combined speed. The instructors extend it charmingly, turn the same way as the birds too, they've learned to circle peacefully with us, and turning against them frightens them out of their own home.
Theo: First into the core sets the turn, follow the birds. And on a ridge, head-on along a slope?
Maya: Both turn away from the slope, both turn right when orientation allows. The key is who has no escape: the pilot with the slope on their left has nowhere to go, so the pilot with the slope on the right gives way, turning out from the hill. Signal early, turn away visibly so the other sees you've seen them, don't leave it to the last few metres just because the lift is better near the rock. And landing, the lowest glider has priority, they have least room to wait. A yellow cross laid on a field means the landing is closed.
Theo: Last area. The exam itself. What does it take?
Maya: Two parts, theory and practical. The theory is one hundred multiple-choice questions across five subjects, aerodynamics, meteorology, law, flying skills and equipment. You need sixteen out of twenty in each, eighty percent. Fail one or two subjects, you re-sit only those; fail more than two, the whole theory again. The Federation issues the licence, mandated by the Federal Office of Civil Aviation, and you appeal an examiner's decision in writing to that office, within thirty days.
Theo: And the practical?
Maya: You arrive with at least forty major flights logged on at least five different sites, certified by an instructor, and the exam launch site doesn't count toward the five. The test is two flights: first, two continuous full circles to the right, in and out on a given line, within twenty seconds; second, a figure of eight, left then right, within thirty seconds. Each, you land within thirty metres of a target, with three attempts. And this is the one place helmet and reserve become compulsory, plus a certified wing matching your registration.
Theo: Forty flights, five sites, two right circles, a figure-eight. One last thing, the responsibilities on the ground.
Maya: The OACS makes us responsible for the wildlife too: keep clear of the reserves shown on that federal viewer, give golden eagles space March to May when they're raising young, and spare chamois and ibex on south slopes in winter. And the plain rules, don't fly over outdoor crowds, don't fly low over roads or ski slopes, don't take off or land on water. The pilot in command carries all of it.
Theo: Alright, land this for me. The spine of the whole legislation paper.
Maya: The Confederation makes the law, the Federal Office of Civil Aviation enforces it, the OACS governs us. Three things are compulsory, insurance, the wing number, the maker's plate, and you carry your certificate and licence every flight. The airspace is a stack, a six-hundred-metre G carpet, then E up to three thousand and fifty in the lowlands or four thousand five hundred and fifty in the Alps, with C and D the controlled zones we stay out of. Check four sources, and the DABS every single day, date and UTC. Fly VFR, with relaxed cloud rules only in the lowest three hundred metres. And right-of-way always favours the wing that can't get out of the way.
Theo: G carpet, the ceilings by region, the daily DABS, VFR cloud clearances, and the less-manoeuvrable wins. I can hold those.
Maya: Then the back of the booklet just became the part that keeps you alive. Catch you in the next episode.
