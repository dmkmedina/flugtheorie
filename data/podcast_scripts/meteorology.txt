# Meteorology
# One turn per line as 'Name: text'. Lines starting with # are ignored.
# Hosts: Maya (instructor) and Theo (curious learner).

Theo: Last episode you signed off with a threat. You said meteorology was where the air starts fighting back. So go on, scare me.
Maya: I'd rather equip you. The honest truth instructors tell every student: three things hurt paraglider pilots, weather-wise. Thunderstorms, the Föhn, and the valley wind. And all three tend to show up on a beautiful-looking day.
Theo: So the sky lies to you.
Maya: The sky tells the truth, you just have to learn its language. This whole episode is about reading it well enough to decide when not to fly. That decision is the real exam.
Theo: Alright. Where do we start?
Maya: At the top. All our weather happens in an astonishingly thin shell of air, a band about as thin, seen from space, as the skin of an onion. It's layered, but the only one we care about is the lowest, the troposphere. It holds almost all the water vapour, so it makes the weather, and it's the only layer we fly in, reaching up to around eleven thousand metres before it hits a ceiling called the tropopause. The air itself is roughly seventy-eight percent nitrogen, twenty-one percent oxygen, and a last percent of leftovers. Learn seventy-eight and twenty-one.
Theo: Now air pressure. I never had a clean picture for that.
Maya: It's the weight of the whole column of air above you pressing down, like water getting heavier the deeper a diver goes. Highest at sea level, and it drops fast low down, slower up high. The standard sea-level value is one thousand and thirteen point two hectopascals. Two points to memorise: at five thousand five hundred metres it's about half of sea level, at eleven thousand about a quarter. So nine hundred and eighty at sea level becomes four hundred and ninety at five thousand five hundred.
Theo: And temperature does the same?
Maya: It cools too, on average about zero point six five degrees per hundred metres, the standard lapse rate. But the real atmosphere bends that rule constantly, and those bends are the whole story of a flying day.
Theo: So the air's just sitting in this thin shell. What stirs it up?
Maya: One engine. The sun. And here's the most important sentence in the episode: the sun does not heat the air directly. The radiation pours straight through the air and heats the ground, and the ground heats the thin layer of air on top of it. Indirect, ground first, air second.
Theo: Why does that detail matter so much?
Maya: Because not every patch of ground heats the air equally, and that's where thermals are born. Dark, dry, rough ground, a ploughed field, rock, asphalt, warms the air strongly. Wet ground, swamp or forest, wastes the energy evaporating water, and bright snow just bounces the sunlight back. So it's never about warm in absolute terms, it's which surface is warmer than its neighbours, and in winter the thermal might be over the bare forest, not the snowy meadow. Angle matters too: morning thermals favour east faces, midday the south slopes, late afternoon the west.
Theo: So a warm layer builds on the ground. Then what?
Maya: Something nudges it loose, a gust, a passing car, a cloud shadow, and a pocket of warm, light air detaches and rises, like a blob in a lava lamp, or a hot-air balloon. That rising bubble is a thermal, and cool air sinking to replace it completes the loop, the whole motion we call convection.
Theo: And how high does the bubble climb? Give me the number.
Maya: As it rises it cools at a perfectly constant rate, one degree for every hundred metres. The dry adiabatic lapse rate, and it never changes, however warm or cold the day. It expands into thinner air, and expanding uses up its energy, so it cools, like the ice-cold blast from a compressed-air can. Push it down and it warms, like a bike pump getting hot.
Theo: One degree per hundred going up. How does that tell me where it stops?
Maya: Compare it to the air around it. The bubble keeps rising while it stays warmer, and lighter, than its surroundings. The moment its temperature matches the air around it, the buoyancy is gone and it stops dead.
Theo: So the day's ceiling is wherever it runs out of being warmer.
Maya: Precisely. We plot the surrounding air's temperature against height on a chart called an emagram, and unlike that clean one-degree line it's messy and unpredictable. In Switzerland just one station launches the sounding balloons for it, at Payerne. A healthy day cools nicely, roughly zero point six to zero point eight degrees per hundred metres up to around three thousand metres. What kills it are two kinds of layer: an isothermal layer, where the temperature holds steady, and an inversion, where it gets warmer with height. Your bubble rises in, finds itself colder and denser than its surroundings, and stops. Inversions and isothermal layers are the invisible ceilings of the sky.
Theo: Where do those ceilings come from?
Maya: The commonest forms overnight: the ground radiates its heat away, cools faster than the air above, and by dawn a shallow cold layer caps thermals until the morning sun burns it off. That's the fog-in-the-valley, sun-on-the-peaks morning, cold at the lake in Zürich while the skiers up top sit in shirtsleeves. Beautiful, but dead for thermals until it lifts.
Theo: You keep mentioning water. Let's do water properly.
Maya: First, the fact that surprises everyone: water vapour is completely invisible. Every cloud, every wisp of fog, even what we call steam, isn't vapour, it's countless tiny droplets of liquid water. So a cloud is just water that's stopped being invisible.
Theo: So how does it stop being invisible?
Maya: Warm air holds a lot of vapour. Cool it and at some point it can't hold it all, and the surplus condenses. The temperature where that begins is the dew point. An air parcel at seventeen degrees with five grams of water per cubic metre is fairly dry, but cool it to about minus seven and that same five grams is now the maximum it can hold, a hundred percent humidity, and cloud forms.
Theo: And how does this hook back into thermals?
Maya: Here's the lovely twist. Once a rising bubble cools to its dew point and water condenses inside it, that condensation releases a little hidden heat, which partly fights the cooling. So a cloudy thermal cools slower, around zero point six per hundred metres instead of one, often giving a second wind right at cloudbase. That same effect is the secret engine of the Föhn later.
Theo: And the cloud on top of the thermal is the famous cumulus?
Maya: Your best friend in the sky. Cumulus is the cauliflower cloud. The small flat-bottomed and medium ones are flags planted on working thermals, base usually between one thousand and four thousand metres. You read the field of them like a map.
Theo: And when does that friend turn into a foe?
Maya: When the day keeps feeding them and they grow. A small cumulus swells into a towering cumulus congestus, dark-based, and that boils up into the monster, the cumulonimbus, the thunderstorm cloud. It towers up to the tropopause, around ten thousand metres, where it flattens into an anvil because it can't punch higher. Inside, the currents are far faster than any paraglider. Hail, lightning, violence.
Theo: Are there other clouds I actually need to name?
Maya: A whole zoo, but here's the trick: two families and a prefix. Cumulus means heaped, unstable air that wants to rise; stratus means a flat layer, stable air settling in. Then the prefix gives height, cirro- is very high, six to ten thousand metres, thin and icy; alto- is mid-level; no prefix means low; and nimbo- means it's raining. So you bolt them together: cirrostratus is that thin high milky veil that puts a coloured halo round the sun.
Theo: Any clouds that are outright warning signs?
Maya: Two. Altocumulus castellanus, little turrets like castle battlements in the morning, mean unstable humid air aloft and often forecast afternoon thunderstorms. And lenticular clouds, smooth lens shapes that sit perfectly still above the mountains while a gale roars through them. They're stationary because strong wind over a mountain sets up a standing wave, and the cloud condenses rising on the wave and evaporates sinking down it, holding its shape while the air pours through. A lenticular means powerful wind aloft, and for us it's a flat-out alarm bell.
Theo: Let's zoom out. The highs and lows, where do those come from?
Maya: Same engine, continental scale. Ground that stays colder than its surroundings, Siberia, a continent in winter, chills the air dense and gives high pressure. Ground that stays warmer, the Sahara in summer, lets it rise and gives low pressure, a depression. On maps these are drawn with isobars, lines joining equal pressure, always corrected to sea level so you can compare a mountain town with a coast.
Theo: How should I picture an isobar map, and how do I get wind out of it?
Maya: Like a hiking map of hills and valleys, but the contours are pressure. A big high is a tall hill, the H marks its summit, with average pressure around one thousand and fifteen, a strong winter high thirty to forty above that, a deep depression nine hundred and fifty-five. And air flows from high to low to even things out, faster the bigger the difference, so packed isobars mean strong wind and widely spaced ones mean light wind. You read strength straight off the spacing.
Theo: I'd expect it to blow straight from high to low.
Maya: You'd think so, but the spinning Earth bends moving air to the right up here, the Coriolis effect, so it runs parallel to the isobars instead, like the swirl when a bath drains. Clockwise around a high, anti-clockwise around a low. A trick: a high is positive, good flying weather, and clockwise is the clock running forward. So to read a wind, decide high or low and lay arrows along the isobars in that rotation. And wind is named for where it comes from: north is zero degrees, east ninety, south one eighty, west two seventy, so one thirty-five is a south-easterly. For speed, double the knots and knock off ten percent, so twenty-five knots is forty-five kilometres an hour.
Theo: Do a high and a low feel different to fly in?
Maya: Very. In a high the air sinks, warms and dries, stable, often an inversion around fifteen hundred to two thousand metres. In a low it rises, cooling and moistening, unsettled. And between them is the pilot's sweet spot, the barometric swamp, a wide zone of in-between pressure with isobars spread far apart. Almost no wind, but unstable and moist, so lovely thermals with a high ceiling, the catch being afternoon thunderstorms.
Theo: That's the big synoptic wind. But inside the Alps the local winds take over.
Maya: They often dominate, and the chief one is the daily valley wind. On a sunny day the narrow upper parts of a valley heat faster than the broad main valley, so pressure drops up there and air flows up the valley to fill in. It kicks in around eleven, peaks mid-afternoon, then fades, and after sunset the slopes cool and the mountain wind drains back down, around six or seven. Strongest in July and August.
Theo: Is it dangerous, or just useful?
Maya: Both, and this is where pilots get caught. The big alpine valleys, Rhône, Rhine, Reuss, gather the flow from dozens of side valleys like the trunk of a tree, and in spring the Rhône valley floor can run fifty to sixty kilometres an hour by mid-afternoon while a few hundred metres up it's calm. Sink into that floor late and it's hammering, so it's often safer to top-land than commit to a screaming valley landing. The valley wind strengthens with height until around two thousand metres, where the prevailing wind takes over.
Theo: Right, you've stalled long enough. Tell me about the Föhn.
Maya: Warm, dry, gusty, one of the deadliest winds we have. Two ingredients: a strong pressure difference across the Alps, and a barrier tall enough to wring the moisture out of the air. Walk over the mountain with me. On the windward side moist air is forced up, cools, condenses, and dumps its water as rain, cooling slowly at that moist rate, around zero point six per hundred metres, piling against the crest into the Föhn wall. Then it spills over, and now the menace: it's lost its water, so pouring down the lee side it's dry, and dry air warms fast, the full one degree per hundred. So it arrives much warmer, drier, stronger and brutally gusty.
Theo: Like a vacuum cleaner pulling air down into the valleys.
Maya: The very image instructors use. It wedges into the lee valleys with ferocious wind at the floor even when your launch feels calm. That's why it kills: with Föhn you get blue sky and gorgeous weather that begs you to fly.
Theo: How do I know it's a Föhn day and not just a nice one?
Maya: The Föhn wall on the upwind ridge, lenticulars sitting still in the lee, suddenly crystal-clear air, lee valleys warming fast, and on the map a sharp kink in the isobars over the main ridge, the Föhnknie. It comes two ways: South Föhn, rain on the southern slopes and deceptive sun in the north; North Föhn, the mirror, sun down in Ticino.
Theo: Is there a hard number that says don't fly?
Maya: Yes, and carry this one: the sea-level pressure difference between Zürich and Lugano. The practical Swiss threshold is four hectopascals. The exam may quote five, but in the real world four is the conservative cut-off, because at four the Föhn already breaks through reliably. Eight is the absolute outer limit only a few very experienced locals touch. So the rule: more than four across the Alps, or a Föhn wall, or sudden warmth and clarity in the valleys, and you do not fly, even if it looks perfect. And beware the dry Föhn, clear blue sky, no wall, no lenticular, just as turbulent. Don't wait for the wall to give you permission.
Theo: This keeps coming back to turbulence. Nail down the types.
Maya: Three, by where they're born, and one golden rule: the stronger the wind, the more turbulence, and the more violent. Mechanical is wind hitting solid obstacles, like those wind-tunnel videos of air breaking up behind a car. Downwind, on the lee side, it tumbles into rotor reaching out up to ten times the obstacle's height. Never fly the lee side of a ridge, never land behind a building.
Theo: And the other two?
Maya: The Venturi effect is the same family: where wind squeezes, over a ridge or into a narrowing valley, it speeds up, fiercest right on a windy peak and gentler a couple of hundred metres lower. So if you fear strong wind, land in an open valley, never a gorge.
Theo: And shear and thermal?
Maya: Shear is the boundary between two layers moving differently, a north-easterly low and a south-westerly aloft, rumbling where they meet, and friction does the same near the ground on take-off and landing. Thermal turbulence is the chop at the edges of bubbles. On a sunny summer afternoon you meet all three in one hour, worst roughly one to five when convection and the valley wind are both fired up.
Theo: Let's talk fronts. What even is a front?
Maya: First, an air mass: a huge region with roughly uniform temperature and humidity, polar maritime cold and wet, or tropical continental hot and dry. They don't mix easily, so where two meet there's a sharp boundary, the front.
Theo: Warm versus cold?
Maya: In both the warmer air rides up over the colder, it's the manner that differs. A warm front slides up gently, so you see it coming from far off and high: wispy cirrus, then thickening cirrostratus and altostratus, the sky going milkier, finally low nimbostratus and steady rain. It ambles across Switzerland at fifteen to twenty-five kilometres an hour over twelve to twenty-four hours. That cloud sequence is your exam giveaway.
Theo: And the cold front is the nasty one.
Maya: The cold front shoves. Cold dense air rams in underneath and throws the warm air violently up, picture the cross-section as a teardrop pushing in low. The cloud is narrower but much taller, a barrier of cumulonimbus marching in from the west, thunder, gusts, hail. It comes at forty to seventy kilometres an hour, twice the speed of a warm front, and the cruel part is the hours ahead can be sunny and tempting, often with a South Föhn running out front. Then the wall arrives.
Theo: So what's the rule when I see one coming?
Maya: One eye on the radar, one on the sky. And if you see a line of storms strung along it, a squall line, that's a categorical no-fly, land immediately even a hundred kilometres away.
Theo: A hundred kilometres? That feels like overkill.
Maya: It isn't, and here's the most underestimated killer in the sport. As a storm matures, cold air dumps out of the cloud like a bucket of water tipped over, and spreads along the ground in every direction. That's the gust front, and it can race twenty to fifty kilometres ahead of the visible storm, into air that looks perfectly calm. It has killed experienced pilots. So the line is simple: any cumulonimbus on the radar within about fifty kilometres, land, and trust your eyes over your phone, because the radar is always fifteen to thirty minutes old.
Theo: And when that fast cold front catches the warm one?
Maya: They merge into an occlusion. The danger there is masked thunderstorms, hidden behind the warm front's grey deck, invisible from below yet still firing out their gusts as they pass.
Theo: Heat storms versus front storms, different things?
Maya: Different drivers and timing. A heat storm is local, sun-fuelled, a humid afternoon where convection grows a cumulus through congestus into a cumulonimbus right over you, so usually late afternoon after the ground's cooked. A front storm is tied to the front, not the clock, so a cold front can fire day or night, and summer cold fronts are especially violent. When a forecast says fresh polar air into hot stuffy summer, read it as thunderstorms, hail, gusts.
Theo: Give me the watch-the-sky checklist for a storm building.
Maya: Watch the cumulus field. Friendly little puffs early, then the warning sequence: bases merge into one big dark base spanning kilometres, the base darkens as the tower blocks the sun, and then the big one, suddenly everything starts lifting, the whole valley going up at once.
Theo: That sounds like it should feel great, lift everywhere.
Maya: It feels great and it means the door is closing, that widespread lift is the storm organising its inflow. See a grey curtain of rain reaching from cloudbase to the ground and it's fully developed, land at once, well clear of the storm.
Theo: One more. There's a thermal that makes no cloud?
Maya: The blue thermal. Convection that never makes a cumulus, because the air's so dry that the bubble runs out of buoyancy before it ever reaches its dew point. Good lift, no cloud marker, so you read the ground and your variometer. And the season: thermals are best April to August, and the snowmelt staggers it, the pre-Alps peaking around May, the high Alps not until August.
Theo: And Switzerland has a greatest-hits list of weather patterns?
Maya: Six. A high on the Alps, stable and often ideal, though watch winter stratus on the plateau and summer storms. The barometric swamp, light wind and great thermals with afternoon storm risk. The Bise, a pronounced north-easterly on the plateau, thirty to fifty kilometres an hour, whose trick is high fog shoved up to fifteen or eighteen hundred metres, leaving the plateau under grey stratus while the Alps bask above. A westerly flow with a low over Scandinavia, good weather in short bursts. And the two Föhn situations we've pulled apart.
Theo: Alright Maya, land the glider. Give me the spine of all this.
Maya: The sun heats the ground, the ground heats the air, and warm bubbles rise as thermals, cooling at a steady one degree per hundred metres. They climb until they hit air as warm as themselves, which is why inversions and isothermal layers are the sky's invisible ceilings. Cumulus marks the good thermals, the cumulonimbus is the monster. Highs spin clockwise, lows anti-clockwise, wind parallel to the isobars. The valley wind builds through the afternoon, and the Föhn comes dressed as beautiful weather.
Theo: And the three killers wearing that beautiful weather: thunderstorms, the Föhn, the valley wind.
Maya: Those three. Thunderstorms, with that gust front reaching fifty kilometres into clear air. The Föhn, more than four hectopascals between Zürich and Lugano, don't fly. And the valley wind, calm up top, hammering on the valley floor.
Theo: So the real skill isn't knowing the clouds. It's deciding when to keep the wing in the bag.
Maya: That's the whole episode in one sentence. Reading the sky is exam knowledge. Choosing not to fly on a gorgeous-looking day is airmanship, and the good pilots are the ones who keep matching theory to the real sky until the right call becomes instinct.
Theo: Onion-skin atmosphere, ground-up heating, the invisible ceilings, isobars like a hiking map, and three killers in disguise. That I can carry.
Maya: Then you can read a flying day. Catch you in the next episode, where we leave the sky and come back to the rules, the airspace and the law.
